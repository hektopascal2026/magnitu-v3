1. What is Magnitu?

Magnitu is a personal relevance engine that learns what you care about and scores every incoming article, email, and legal notice on a scale from 0 to 100.

It does this by watching your judgments: you read entries and assign them one of four labels. Over time, Magnitu builds a model to predict your judgment on entries you haven't seen yet.

The Four Labels

These are the categories Magnitu learns to predict:

Investigation Lead The highest-value category. Points to a story worth pursuing, evidence of wrongdoing, or connections between actors.
Important Relevant to your beat and worth reading, but not an actionable lead on its own.
Background Tangentially related or good to know, but not something you'd act on today.
Noise Irrelevant to your work, such as press releases, off-topic content, or duplicates.

2. The Learning Loop

Magnitu improves through a repeating five-step cycle. Each cycle sharpens the model's accuracy.

1

Sync

Pull new entries from the main Seismo feed aggregator. You can run a standard "Sync" for quick incremental updates or a "Full Sync" for source-by-source backfill and embedding completion. Both run as background jobs with live progress bars.

2

Label

Read entries and assign them one of the four categories. Each label serves as a concrete training example.

3

Train

Magnitu builds a completely new model from all your historical labels. You need at least {{ config.min_labels_to_train or 20 }} labels to start, but more data yields better accuracy.

4

Score & Push

The model scores every entry and sends those numbers, along with a lightweight keyword "recipe", to Seismo.

5

Review & Correct

Check the Top 30 page to review the model's highest-ranked entries. If it ranked a press release as a lead, reclassify it—these corrections are the highest-value training data because they target exact areas of model confusion.

3. How Magnitu Reads Text

Before Magnitu can learn from your labels, it needs to convert articles into a mathematical format. It relies on two concurrent systems.

The Language Model & Meaning Vectors

Transformer Architecture: Magnitu uses a pre-trained language model (defaulting to {{ config.transformer_model_name or 'xlm-roberta-base' }}). With 278 million parameters trained across 100 languages, it inherently understands what words mean before seeing your labels.

Meaning Vectors: For each article, the model produces a "meaning vector"—a 768-dimensional list of numbers that captures the article's concept. This enables cross-language understanding, meaning "Untersuchung" and "investigation" produce similar fingerprints.

Input Construction: The text passed to the embedder is built from the title (emphasised by repeating it once), the description, and a capped excerpt of the body (500 characters). It also prepends structured context from Seismo: source_type, source_name, and source_category.

Caching: Embedding runs once per article during sync. If the main text or Seismo context fields change later, the cached vector is automatically dropped and recomputed on the next sync.

Training: From Understanding to Judgment

The Classifier Head: Every time you train, Magnitu builds a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) from scratch, trained on your cached transformer embeddings.

What It Learns: The language model provides the semantic understanding, while your labels teach the MLP which vectors correspond to which priorities.

The Recipe & Knowledge Distillation

Knowledge Distillation: Because Seismo runs on PHP, it cannot execute Python transformer models directly. To bridge this, Magnitu trains a simpler TF-IDF student model to mimic the transformer's predictions.

The Exported Recipe: A lightweight JSON summary (the "recipe") is extracted from this student model. It contains weighted text features (single words, bigrams, and trigrams) and maintains both strong positive and strong negative signals per class.

Live Scoring: This recipe is pushed to Seismo, allowing its PHP engine to evaluate new incoming articles in milliseconds by matching words against the recipe's weights.

4. Relevance Scores & Measuring Quality

Every entry gets a relevance score from 0 to 1 (shown as a percentage).

Probability & Calibration: The model estimates the probability of each label. These probabilities are calibrated using a simple temperature fit on a held-out slice of your training data to prevent overconfidence.

Weighted Average: The final relevance score is a weighted composite of these calibrated probabilities:

Investigation Lead Weight 1.0
Important Weight 0.66
Background Weight 0.33
Noise Weight 0.0

Measuring Quality

When training, Magnitu holds back ~20% of your labels as a test set to calculate Accuracy. It also calculates an F1 Score, which is crucial because it penalizes models that get high accuracy simply by calling everything "noise" (class imbalance).

5. Workspaces: Mothership, Satellites, and Profiles

Magnitu is designed for newsrooms where teams share a data pool but have diverging beats.

The Mothership: All profiles share a single, global connection to the main Seismo instance. You sync entries once, and embedding computation runs once per entry for all profiles.

Multi-Profile Architecture: One Magnitu instance can run multiple isolated profiles. Each profile trains its own model on its own labels and manages its own recipe.

Lightweight Satellites: Each profile can push its specific scores to a dedicated "satellite" Seismo installation (one PHP install per topic). The satellite holds its own database for Magnitu scores and state, while entries still stream from the mothership.

Sharing (.magnitu files): Models can be exported as a zip archive containing the manifest.json, labels.json, model.joblib, calibration.json, and recipe.json.

Forking: You can "fork" a team's base model. This gives you the team's shared baseline judgments (e.g., press releases = noise), which you can then customize by relabeling entries for your specific beat.

6. Advanced Labeling Strategies

The Smart Queue

Magnitu mixes several strategies to surface entries that will improve the model fastest:

Uncertain

Entries where the model's probabilities are evenly spread.

Conflict

Entries where the deep local model and the lightweight PHP recipe disagree on the predicted label.

Diverse

Entries from underrepresented source categories to broaden the model's experience.

New

The most recent unlabeled entries.

Reasoning Notes

You can optionally write a short note explaining why an entry received a label.

The Effect: The deep model does not read this text. Instead, the recipe distiller extracts matching keywords from your reasoning and boosts them in the Seismo recipe. If enabled in settings, the presence of a reasoning note increases that row's training weight (Reasoning-Weight Boost).

Legislation Monitoring (The Swiss Use Case)

Foreign legislation rarely mentions Switzerland by name. Magnitu learns to spot these hidden impacts by recognizing patterns across languages:

  • Exclusion language: "EU or EEA", "Binnenmarkt".
  • Third-country provisions: "Drittstaaten", "equivalence".
  • Compliance triggers: "CE-Kennzeichnung", "Konformitätsbewertung".

Workflow: Use the "Legislation" source filter to label 50–100 legal items, adding reasoning notes to boost terms. Check the Learned Legal Patterns panel on the Model page to ensure the model is picking up the right signals.

7. Tuning & Advanced Settings

Settings are divided into global (shared) and per-profile knobs.

Profile Settings (Saved Per Profile)

Minimum Labels to Train Default 20. The safety gate before a model can generalize.
Recipe Top Keywords Default 200. Number of words/phrases pushed to the Seismo recipe.
Alert Threshold Default 0.75. The score above which Seismo highlights an entry as an alert.
Lead Discovery Blend Default 0, Max 0.25. Mixes extra investigation_lead probability into the final relevance score.
Label Time-Decay Default Off. Sets a half-life in days so older labels count less during training.
Reasoning-Weight Boost Default Off. Assigns a heavier training multiplier (e.g., 1.3–1.5x) to labels with reasoning.

Global Settings (Shared Across All Profiles)

Legal-Signal Patterns: A user-configured list of regex/phrases (e.g., 5-20 phrases like Drittland or Binnenmarkt). Matches are prepended to the embedded text as signals=... and aggressively boosted in the Seismo recipe. Note: Changing this list invalidates all cached embeddings globally.

8. Known Biases & Limitations

Text Length Magnitu normalizes keyword weights in the recipe to compensate for length bias. The local transformer caps body excerpts at 500 characters.
Source Bias The recipe includes source weights (e.g. RSS toward noise). The deep model ignores source types unless they use distinctive language.
Class Imbalance Magnitu automatically up-weights rare classes (like leads) during training to prevent them from being ignored.
Recency Bias Unless Label Time-Decay is enabled, all labels hold equal weight regardless of age.
